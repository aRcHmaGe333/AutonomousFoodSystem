# Assumptions Sheet

## Physical assumptions

- corridor geometry can reduce positional uncertainty enough to simplify milking interaction
- passive or semi-passive indexing can replace part of the complexity of a robotic positioning system
- cradle-based interaction can be made mechanically reliable enough for repeated use
- workable corridor angles, widths, hoof-indexing geometry, and teat-positioning tolerances can be defined without collapsing the concept into full robotic complexity
- cradle pressure limits and pinch-point control can be kept within a defendable mechanical safety envelope

## Welfare assumptions

- calmer, lower-force handling can be achieved through geometry and operating conditions
- voluntary or low-resistance entry is plausible under the proposed setup
- the interaction model does not introduce unacceptable welfare risks
- reward-feed and training protocols can be defined without creating unrealistic handling dependence
- refusal rates, agitation rates, and stress indicators can remain low enough to preserve throughput assumptions

## Sanitation assumptions

- a practical sanitation cycle can coexist with throughput requirements
- zone separation can materially reduce contamination risk
- verification steps can be operationalized without unrealistic labor overhead
- sanitation chemistry, contact time, rinse behavior, and verification method can be specified concretely enough for pilot design
- ATP or CFU verification can be integrated without making the cycle operationally unrealistic

## Waste and digestion assumptions

- manure handling and digestion can be integrated without overwhelming operational complexity
- digestate can become a meaningful nutrient-loop output rather than a purely theoretical byproduct
- digester sizing, volatile-solids handling, pathogen reduction, and nutrient polishing can be described with a plausible mass-balance path

## Economic assumptions

- intervention rates may be reduced enough to matter economically
- complexity reduction may offset added subsystem integration cost
- the design does not become uneconomic before it becomes operationally credible
- anomaly rates, maintenance hours, staffing burden, and opex per liter can stay within a range that keeps the concept worth piloting

## Measurement requirements implied by these assumptions

- positional variance and attachment success
- entry refusal and welfare or stress indicators
- ATP or CFU sanitation verification and contamination transfer checks
- methane yield, volatile-solids destruction, pathogen reduction, and nutrient profile
- intervention rate, maintenance hours, staffing assumptions, and opex per liter

## Claim discipline

None of the assumptions above should be treated as proven.
They are the assumptions external reviewers are being asked to attack, qualify, or support.
If a claim cannot yet be bounded by tolerances, protocol details, or measurement plans, it should remain softened in outward-facing material.
