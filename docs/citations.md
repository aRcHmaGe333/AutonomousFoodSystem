Q&A: Can Scope 3 Accounting Evolve to Advance Decarbonization Progress?


This article, a Q&A moderated by Kristen Sullivan of Deloitte, features insights from Cynthia Cummis, Laura Draucker, and Holly Lahd—experts involved in the original development of the Greenhouse Gas Protocol’s Scope 3 Standard. First created in 2011, the Scope 3 Standard is a globally recognized framework for measuring greenhouse gas emissions across a company’s entire value chain. The discussion centers on how the standard has evolved—and how it must continue to evolve—to support corporate decarbonization. The experts agree that while Scope 3 accounting has helped companies identify emission "hot spots" and set reduction targets, progress has been slowed by data availability challenges, performance tracking limitations, and uncertainty over what reductions can be formally accounted for. They suggest enhancements such as introducing a “second ledger” to record difficult-to-trace emission reduction impacts, and reshaping the standard to accommodate real-world investment and decarbonization efforts. They also emphasize that companies shouldn’t wait for perfect data to act, and that aligning accounting standards with solutions-driven climate action is crucial as the world moves toward net-zero goals. A revised Scope 3 Standard could better serve regulators, investors, and companies if it unifies fragmented guidance and drives more substantial emissions reductions.

ISO, Greenhouse Gas Protocol to Align on Carbon Accounting Rules


The International Organization for Standardization (ISO) and the Greenhouse Gas (GHG) Protocol have announced a significant collaboration to harmonize carbon accounting standards. They aim to create a unified framework aligning on terminology, measurement, and reporting of greenhouse gas emissions. This move addresses challenges arising from fragmented reporting standards used globally, which currently include ISO’s ISO 1406x series, the GHG Protocol, the Task Force on Climate-Related Financial Disclosures (TCFD), and the Carbon Disclosure Project (CDP). The GHG Protocol is widely utilized, with 97% of S&P 500 companies reporting emissions to the CDP using its standard in 2023. The joint framework seeks to streamline carbon accounting, facilitate transparent benchmarking of progress toward net-zero goals, and support investors with reliable data for climate-focused capital allocation. However, environmental groups like ECOS emphasize that the new standards must be science-based, transparent, and inclusive, warning against any dilution of rigor. While no timeline has been provided, stakeholders stress the importance of publicly accessible, credible standards to ensure meaningful climate action.
