# Packaging guidance — reusable containers & replacing cardboard

Purpose
-------
Guidance for replacing single-use cardboard and designing reusable packaging for food and non-food goods. Covers container shapes/sizes, materials, cleaning, racks vs nested returns, tracking, and lifecycle considerations.

Why replace cardboard?
----------------------
- Cardboard is resource-intensive (wood, water), creates waste streams, and produces methane in landfills. 
- Reusable systems reduce material extraction and labour for (a relatively uncertain process of) recycling and can lower lifecycle emissions when used enough times.

Scope
-----
- Prepared food, groceries, consumer goods, parts and small appliances.
- Multiple container families: meal trays, bulk bins, modular crates, padded inserts for fragile goods.

Design priorities
-----------------
- Safety & cleanliness (food-grade where needed)
- Reusability and reparability
- Stackability and transport efficiency
- Ease of automated handling and washing
- Traceability and lifecycle accounting

Container families & use-cases
------------------------------
- Meal containers: shallow, rectangular trays (250 ml, 500 ml, 1 L).
- Mixed-goods crates: modular crates with configurable internal partitions and padded inserts for fragile items.
- Bulk bins: stackable 20–50 L crates for grocery items and restocking.
- Padded inserts: removable foam or corrugated plastic inserts for fragile consumer goods.

Materials & tradeoffs
---------------------
- Metals (316 stainless): high lifetime, good for hot-food/industrial use, heavier and costlier.
- Aluminum (anodized): lightweight, consider coating durability for acidic foods.
- Plastics (PP, HDPE, Tritan): cheap, lightweight, easy to mold; pick high-reuse grades and ensure recycling stream compatibility.
- Corrugated plastic (Flute polypropylene): reusable corrugated sheets for inserts—better lifetime than cardboard.

Replacing cardboard strategies
-----------------------------
- Use modular crates with padded inserts that can be reused across SKUs; keep inserts as consumables that are cheap to replace but recyclable.
- Use collapsible crates and consolidated returns to reduce empty run volume.
- For single-item shipments, pack into modular envelope-like soft containers that fold and are sanitizable.

Cleaning & depot operations
---------------------------
- Centralized CIP/rack washing for depots handling many returns; automated tunnels for high throughput.
- Distributed wash-and-return for local pickup hubs; design containers for robust manual washing.

Economic considerations
-----------------------
- Calculate reuse breakeven: cost of reusable unit / (cost of single-use cardboard package) = required reuses.
- Track loss/theft rates and include replacement capex in the model.

Traceability & incentives
-------------------------
- Use RFID/QR to track return rates and apply incentives (credits, deposits) to drive return compliance.
- Integrate data into order & logistics systems to predict refill routes and avoid empty returns.

Pilot defaults (recommended)
----------------------------
- Grocery/parts pilot: modular PP crates (20 L) with modular inserts; collapsible return crates for transport.
- Meal pilot: PP meal trays (500 ml/1 L) with silicone gasket lids; depot racks for washing.
